Shuǐ Jiǎo Recipe

Description

I first came across shuǐ jiǎo (水餃) when my wife was taking me on a journey through Chinese cuisine. As you can probably tell if you've read any other articles on this site, she has basically been my guide through most of my food journey. Shuǐ jiǎo, which literally translates to water dumplings, are very popular in Taiwan, China, and much of Asia. They quickly became one of my favorite foods to order at Chinese restaurants, and I wanted to impress my wife by learning how to order them in her native tongue. This was where the trouble began.

A little background, my wife is fluent in Mandarin, and I took a language class in college trying to pick up a few phrases. It was the only class that I nearly failed, and without her help, I surely would have been defeated. After I became a little more comfortable with some simple words, I would practice speaking Chinese by attempting to order off the menu at restaurants. More than once, I tried ordering shuǐ jiǎo dumplings (水餃), but I accidentally said "shuì jiào" (睡覺), which means "to sleep."

So the waitress had asked me, "你要什麼" ("What would you like to order?"), and I replied: "我要睡覺," which means "I want to sleep." The poor girl looked at me with a look of complete confusion, to which my wife giggled and politely corrected the order. This was not the last time this happened to me, nor was it even close to my most embrassing moment in my journey studying Chinese. To this day, I still routinely mix up words or say something in the wrong context that can return a strange look, but it's all part of the ball game. Similarly, you may struggle to make these dumplings look perfect, or they may fall apart if you haven't properly shaped the dough, but the most important thing is to have fun and enjoy the ride. Good luck with your 水餃! (not 睡覺...)

Ingredients

  • 60 dumpling wrappers (can be home made or store-bought)
  • 250g pork mince
  • 1 tablespoon oyster sauce
  • 1 tablespoon light soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons sesame oil
  • 1/4 teaspoon white pepper
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 2 teaspoons grated ginger root
  • 1 tablespoon minced garlic
  • 1 egg
  • 200g finely cut leek
  • 2 tablespoons finely cut cilantro

Steps

  1. Combine the pork, ginger, garlic, white pepper, salt, oyster sauce, soy sauce, and egg into a mixing bowl
  2. Mix well until the ingredients are in a paste, stringy-like form
  3. Combine sesame oil, cilantro, and leek in another bowl. Stir until all ingredients are mixed
  4. Add the cilantro and leek mix into the bowl with the pork mix
  5. Stir to combine all ingredients into a good mix
  6. Pleace a teaspoon of dumpling filling into the middle of a dumpling wrapper
  7. Lift the sides of the paper over the top of the mix, and seal with a little water
  8. Pinch the sides of the dumpling paper together, and slightly fold them in a Z-shape to create the desired look while sealing the dumplings together
  9. This may take a little practice, and be careful not to tear your dumpling paper
  10. Boil the dumplings until pork is cooked
  11. Serve with light soy sauce (can also add vinegar, chili oil, etc to desired taste)

This recipe is from my mother-in-law, and she makes some of the best 水餃 I've ever had. She had a business idea of opening a dumpling shop in Whistler (more as a retirement hobby), and I'm sure it would have taken off. There's nothing like warm dumplings after a long day of skiing. Well...except for maybe some poutine... but we'll leave it at that. If these dumplings are good enough to fill me up after a long day of powder and double diamonds, they are good enough for any occasion you are likely to come across.
