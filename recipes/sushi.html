Sushi Recipe

Description

Sushi is probably my favorite type of food. It is healthy, fun to eat, and (most importantly) delicious. I didn't like sushi as a kid because I was freaked out by the idea of eating raw fish. In college, my wife told me I needed to branch out, so I tried a California roll for first time, and it slowly grew on me. Fast forward nearly 10 years later, and I chose Nobu in Newport Beach for my 30th birthday celebration dinner (would highly recommend by the way).

If you've read any of my other articles, you'll probably already know that I am no Michelin Star chef. My wife once told me I should try out for the popular reality TV cooking show "Worst Cooks in America." All jokes aside, I've never worried about my lack of cooking skills because I prefer to focus on other skills instead. However, when I became a dad, I suddenly felt a great fear of being the father who couldn't cook a nice meal for his family. Sure, I'd love to have my wife's cooking every day for the rest of my life, and I would die a happy man, but I didn't want to be a man who is completely incapable of preparing a nice meal for his family every so often. So I decided to learn to make sushi, because it seemed like a challenge, and I was curious to see how the sushi chefs actually prepare those tuna rolls for us behind the scenes.

After much trial and error, watching a bunch of cooking shows, and making sushi several times, I have compiled the following recipe for making delicious sushi rolls.

Ingredients

Serves 4 people:

  • 3 cups sushi rice
  • 3 tablespoons sushi vinegar
  • sushi seaweed paper
  • meat for rolls (salmon, tuna, yellow tail, smelt eggs, imitation crab, lobster, unagi, blue fin, etc)
  • other vegetables/etc to add into rolls (cucumber, carrot, tamago, cream cheese, tempura, etc)
  • spicy japanese mayo
  • pickled ginger

Steps

  1. Wash the rice at least 3 times and strain until the water runs clear
  2. Cook 3 cups of rice in a rice cooker
  3. When rice is finished, add 3 cups of sushi vinegar to the rice, sprinkle evenly
  4. Toss the rice to evenly disperse the sushi vinegar
  5. Make a ball of rice the size of a baseball
  6. Shape the ball of rice into the shape of a cylinder
  7. Place the cylinder of rice on a piece of sushi seaweed paper
  8. Using your fingertips, gently push the rice to the edges of the seaweed paper, until the entire paper is evenly covered with rice. Be careful not to tear the paper. Feel free to add more rice onto the seaweed if necessary
  9. Flip over the seaweed paper with the rice on it so that the rice is facing downwards and the blank seaweed side is face upwards
  10. Evenly spread some spicy Japanese mayo on the blank side of the seaweed paper
  11. Cut the sushi meat into long, relatively thin pieces
  12. Place the long strips of meat on top of the surface that has the mayo on it. Place the strips of meat close together and in the middle of the seaweed paper to make it easier to roll
  13. Add any vegetables or other desired ingredients along side the meat
  14. Carefully roll the seaweed paper around the meat and other ingredients to wrap them up inside the sushi roll. This may take some practice at first, but it's easy once you get the hang of it
  15. Cover the sushi roll with plastic wrap
  16. Use a sushi roller to pack the sides of the roll, and gently compress the roll to make it all pack together nicely
  17. Remove the plastic wrap
  18. If you are using smelt eggs or any rice seasoning, place some on the surface you are rolling the sushi on, and roll the sushi roll over the smelt eggs or rice seasoning to make a nice layer of the toppings on the roll
  19. Using a large cooking knife, carefully cut the roll into 8 pieces. It helps to wet the blade with water before cutting the roll to make the cut more smooth and precise
  20. Serve with a side of wasabi, pickled ginger, and a light soy sauce

*Optional recommendation: Real wasabi root. This can usually be found in authentic Japanese supermarkets, but not all of them carry it. It is very expensive due to lack of supply in Japan, but the taste is absolutely worth it. Most people are unaware that the standard wasabi included with most dishes at restaurants and with grocery-bought sushi is basically horseradish with some wasabi flavoring, and it is nothing close to the real thing. The reason for this is the expense, and most customers are not interesting in paying extra just to have a different kind of spicy sauce with their meal. However, if you are lucky enough to find the true wasabi root, I would highly recommend trying it at least once. It is a fun experience to grate the wasabi into a paste, and it can make for a fun party feature if you are hosting guests.

*Note: Please follow all safety guidelines regarding the consumption of raw meats or other uncooked ingredients to ensure your safety and the safety of your guests

Enjoy!
